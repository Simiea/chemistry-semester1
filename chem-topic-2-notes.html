Section 2.1

Types of Materials

All solid materials result from intramolecular forces (primary bonds) between atoms or ions. There are four types of solid materials classified by their bonding and structure.

⚙️ Metallic Solids
Formed between: metal atoms only.
Structure: Regular lattice of positive metal cations surrounded by a "sea" of delocalised electrons.
Examples: Magnesium, tin, copper.
Bond: Electrostatic attraction between cations and delocalised electrons.
🔴 Ionic Solids
Formed between: metallic and non-metallic atoms.
Structure: Geometric lattice of alternating cations (+) and anions (−) in fixed ratios.
Examples: Sodium chloride (NaCl), nickel oxide.
Bond: Electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.
🔵 Covalent Molecular Solids
Formed between: non-metallic atoms.
Structure: Discrete molecules interacting via intermolecular forces.
Examples: Sucrose, iodine.
Bond: Sharing of valence electron pairs between non-metal atoms.
💎 Covalent Network Solids
Formed between: non-metallic atoms.
Structure: Each atom forms covalent bonds with ALL nearest neighbours — extending through the entire solid.
Examples: Diamond, graphite, silica (sand).
Bond: Covalent bonds throughout the network lattice.

Melting Points by Bonding Type

For a solid to melt, vibrations must exceed the intramolecular or intermolecular attractive forces. Stronger bonds = higher melting point.

Section 2.1 — Electrical Conductivity

Electrical Conductivity as Evidence for Bonding

Electrical conductivity — the ease with which electric charge passes through a material — is a useful tool for classifying bonding type. It depends on the presence of free charged particles (electrons or ions).

Bonding TypeSolidMoltenAqueous Solution
🔩 MetallicYesYes— (not dissolved)
🔴 IonicNo*YesYes
💎 Covalent NetworkNo**No—
🔵 Covalent MolecularNoNoNo

* Ionic solids have fixed ions — no movement possible.
** Exception: Graphite — delocalised electrons between layers allow excellent conductivity.

💎 Graphite — The Exception
Graphite is a covalent network solid where each carbon bonds to only three adjacent carbon atoms. The fourth valence electron is delocalised and can move freely between the graphite layers — allowing graphite to conduct electricity excellently, unlike all other covalent networks.
Section 2.2

The Octet Rule & Ion Formation

Most atoms prefer to have 8 valence electrons (octet). To achieve this, atoms gain, lose, or share electrons. Noble gases (Group VIII) already have a full octet and are non-reactive.

➕ Cations (Positive Ions)
Formed when: Metal atoms lose valence electrons.

Why metals? Metals have large atomic radii — valence electrons are far from the nucleus and weakly attracted. Low ionisation energies.

Charge: Equal to the number of electrons lost = Group number (for Groups I–III).

Examples: Li → Li⁺ + e⁻  ·  Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻  ·  Al → Al³⁺ + 3e⁻

Naming: Name of the parent atom + "cation" (or just the element name).
➖ Anions (Negative Ions)
Formed when: Non-metal atoms gain electrons.

Why non-metals? High ionisation energies (hard to remove electrons) + high electron affinity (tendency to attract electrons).

Charge: Equal to the number of electrons gained = 8 minus Group number (for Groups V–VII).

Examples: F + e⁻ → F⁻  ·  O + 2e⁻ → O²⁻  ·  N + 3e⁻ → N³⁻

Naming: Element name with suffix changed to "-ide" (fluoride, oxide, nitride).

📊 Ionisation Energy Trends
Across a period: ionisation energy increases (stronger nuclear attraction as atomic radius decreases).
Down a group: ionisation energy decreases (electrons are further from nucleus, weaker attraction).
Metals typically have low ionisation energies (easily lose electrons). Noble gases have the highest (don't form ions normally).
Section 2.2 — Bond Energy

Bond Formation & Energy

Bond formation always releases energy. Breaking bonds always requires energy. Whether a reaction is exothermic or endothermic depends on the net energy change.

🔥 Exothermic Reaction
Energy released (forming bonds) > Energy required (breaking bonds).

Products have lower enthalpy than reactants. Energy is released to surroundings as heat.

Example: Non-metal gaining an electron:
Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻   ΔH = −349 kJ/mol

Energy profile: reactants higher → products lower.
❄️ Endothermic Reaction
Energy released (forming bonds) < Energy required (breaking bonds).

Products have higher enthalpy than reactants. Energy is absorbed from surroundings.

Example: Metal losing an electron:
Na → Na⁺ + e⁻   ΔH = +496 kJ/mol

Energy profile: reactants lower → products higher.
⚡ Activation Energy (Ea)
Activation energy is the minimum energy that must be provided to start a chemical reaction. Even exothermic reactions require an initial energy input (Ea) to break existing bonds before new, more stable bonds can form. Activation energy is measured in joules per mole (J/mol). The enthalpy (ΔH) describes the overall energy change, not the activation energy.
Section 2.2 — Metallic Bonding

Metallic Bonding & Properties

Metals have large atomic radii — valence electrons are weakly held and become delocalised, shared between all neighbouring atoms. This creates a "sea" of electrons around a regular lattice of positive metal cations.

🔩 The Metallic Bond
Strong electrostatic attraction between the positively charged metal cations and the sea of delocalised electrons holds the metallic lattice together. Although cations repel each other, the delocalised electrons act as "glue" holding them together in a dense, 3D regular lattice arranged in layers.

Physical Properties of Metals — Explained

🔨
Hardness
Strong electrostatic forces resist compression. Layers can slide over each other. Alloys are harder than pure metals — different-sized atoms distort the lattice, requiring more force to slide layers.
🔧
Malleability
Ability to withstand compressive stress without breaking. Layers of cations slide over each other but delocalised electrons maintain attraction — bonds remain unbroken. Can be enhanced by heating.
🪢
Ductility
Ability to withstand tensile stress (stretching). Can be drawn into wires. The cation-electron attraction stretches but does not break — bonds remain intact.
⚡
Electrical Conductivity
Delocalised electrons move freely — toward positive electrode, away from negative. This constitutes an electric current. Silver has the greatest electrical conductivity of all metals.
🌡️
Thermal Conductivity
Delocalised electrons vibrate more rapidly when heated, causing more spontaneous collisions — heat is rapidly distributed throughout the metal.
✨
Lustre
Delocalised electrons absorb and re-emit photons — electrons jump to higher energy levels then return, emitting light. The wavelengths emitted give metals their characteristic shiny appearance.
🧊
High Melting/Boiling Point
Strong electrostatic attraction requires significant energy to overcome. Greater cation charge (higher group) AND smaller atomic radius = higher melting point. E.g., Al (660°C, 3+) vs Mg (650°C, 2+).
⚖️
Density
Metal cations are closely packed in the lattice due to electrostatic attraction. Density depends on cation mass, radius, and packing arrangement. Metals generally have high but varied densities.
Why Aluminium Has a Higher Melting Point Than Magnesium
Al (Group III) forms Al³⁺ — greater charge than Mg²⁺ (Group II) → stronger electrostatic attraction → higher melting point (660°C vs 650°C).
However, Mg has a larger atomic radius than Al (radius increases right to left) → nucleus is further from delocalised electrons → weaker attraction → slightly lower melting point.
Section 2.2 — Ionic Bonding

Ionic Bonding

When a metal atom transfers electrons to a non-metal, oppositely charged ions form. The electrostatic attraction between these ions is the ionic bond, forming an ionic compound (salt).

🔴 How Ionic Bonds Form — Two Steps
Step 1: Electron transfer — metal loses valence electrons (forming cation); non-metal gains them (forming anion).
Step 2: Ionic bond — electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions. Energy is released in this step.

Ions arrange in a continuous 3D lattice that maximises cation-anion attraction and minimises like-charge repulsion. The strength of attraction increases with greater charge magnitude on the ions.

Properties of Ionic Compounds

💎
Hard but Brittle
Strong ionic bonds create a hard, stable lattice. BUT when stress shifts layers, like charges align → repulsion causes the crystal to shatter along a smooth plane (brittle). Unlike metals, layers cannot simply slide.
🌡️
High Melting/Boiling Point
Strong electrostatic forces between ions require significant energy to overcome. Higher ion charges = stronger attraction = higher melting point. E.g., MgO (Mg²⁺, O²⁻) has a higher mp than NaCl (Na⁺, Cl⁻).
⚡
Conductivity
Solid: ions in fixed positions — cannot conduct.
Molten or dissolved (aqueous): ions are free to move — conduct electricity.
Higher charge ions carry more current. All ionic compounds are electrolytes.
Comparing Electrolyte Conductivity
MgSO₄ → Mg²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ (doubly charged ions → higher current than NaCl's Na⁺/Cl⁻)
Na₂SO₄ → 2Na⁺ + SO₄²⁻ (more Na⁺ ions than NaCl + SO₄²⁻ carries more charge than Cl⁻)
Section 2.2 — Ionic Formulae

Writing Empirical Formulae for Ionic Compounds

Ionic compounds are electrically neutral — total positive charge must equal total negative charge. The empirical formula shows the simplest ratio of ions.

1
Identify the charges on the cation and anion from the periodic table or group number.
2
If charges are equal (magnitude): the ratio is 1:1 → write cation then anion.
3
If charges are not equal: find the lowest common multiple (LCM) of both charges. The number needed of each ion to reach the LCM becomes the subscript.
4
Write the formula: cation first, then anion with correct subscripts. For polyatomic ions, use brackets when a subscript >1 applies.

⭐ Transition Metal Oxidation States
Transition metals can form ions with multiple possible charges (e.g., Fe²⁺ or Fe³⁺). The oxidation state is written in Roman numerals in brackets after the element name: Iron(II) vs Iron(III). This is essential to distinguish the different ionic compounds they form.

Section 2.2 — Covalent Bonding

Covalent Bonding

Non-metallic atoms share valence electron pairs to complete their octets. The electrostatic attraction between the shared electron pair and the positive nuclei of both atoms is the covalent bond.

—
Single Bond
1 bonding pair (2 electrons)
Weakest covalent bond. Group VII elements (e.g. Cl₂, F₂). Each atom shares 1 electron.
═
Double Bond
2 bonding pairs (4 electrons)
Intermediate strength. Group VI elements (e.g. O₂). Each atom shares 2 electrons.
≡
Triple Bond
3 bonding pairs (6 electrons)
Strongest covalent bond. Group V elements (e.g. N₂). Each atom shares 3 electrons. Very high energy to break.
📐 Determining the Central Atom
The central atom has the highest covalence (can form the most bonds) and usually has the lowest electronegativity. Hydrogen and Group VII atoms are rarely central. For example, in NH₃, nitrogen is central (can form 3 bonds); hydrogen atoms surround it.

Section 2.2 — Lewis Structures

Drawing Lewis Structures

Lewis (electron dot) diagrams show all valence electrons — bonding pairs (as lines) and non-bonding (lone) pairs (as dots). Both bonding and lone pairs must be shown.

1
Write the chemical formula and identify all atoms present.
2
Count total valence electrons from all atoms (use group numbers).
3
Determine bonds needed — how many electrons does each atom need to reach 8? Each bond uses 2 electrons (one from each atom).
4
Identify the central atom — highest covalence, lowest electronegativity (not H, rarely Group VII).
5
Complete octets around outer atoms first with lone pairs.
6
Place remaining electrons on central atom as lone pairs.
7
Check: total electrons in diagram = total valence electrons counted in step 2. Each bond = single line (—), double bond = (=), triple bond (≡).
Worked Example — Ammonia (NH₃)
Valence electrons: N=5, H=1×3 = 8 total.
N needs 3 bonds (needs 3 more electrons). Each H needs 1 bond.
Central atom: N (highest covalence). 3 N−H bonds used. Remaining 2 electrons = 1 lone pair on N.
Lewis structure: N with 3 single bonds to H atoms and 1 lone pair on N.
Section 2.2 — Bond Polarity

Polar and Non-Polar Covalent Bonds

When two atoms share electrons, the electronegativity difference between them determines how equally the electrons are shared — and whether the bond is polar or non-polar.

⚖️ Non-Polar Covalent Bond
Same or negligible electronegativity difference between bonding atoms.

Electrons shared equally — no partial charges. No dipole.

Examples:
Cl₂ — identical atoms, equal sharing.
C−H bond — C (2.5) vs H (2.1) = difference of 0.4 → functionally non-polar.
🔋 Polar Covalent Bond
Significant electronegativity difference between bonding atoms.

Electrons shift toward more electronegative atom → partial negative charge (δ⁻) on that atom, partial positive (δ⁺) on the other. Creates a dipole moment.

Example: HCl — Cl (3.0) vs H (2.1) = difference of 0.9 → electrons shift toward Cl → Cl is δ⁻, H is δ⁺.
📈 From Covalent to Ionic — A Spectrum
Bond polarity exists on a spectrum. As electronegativity difference increases:
Non-polar covalent (ΔEN ≈ 0) → Polar covalent (ΔEN moderate) → Ionic (ΔEN large)

The greater the polarity, the more ionic character the bond possesses. There is no sharp boundary between polar covalent and ionic bonds.
📋 Factors Affecting Electronegativity
Across a period (→): increases — ionisation energy increases, smaller radius, stronger nuclear attraction on bonding electrons.
Down a group (↓): decreases — larger atomic radius + electron shielding weakens nuclear attraction.
Electron shielding: inner electrons shield the nucleus, reducing its apparent pull on outer/bonding electrons — decreasing electronegativity.
Section 2.2 — Covalent Networks

Covalent Network Solids

Covalent networks extend covalent bonds throughout the entire solid — there are no discrete molecules. The size is not fixed. Represented by empirical formulae with subscript n.

💎 Diamond
Element: Carbon only.
Bonding: Each C bonds to four adjacent C atoms (tetrahedral).
Formula: (C)n
Properties: Hardest known substance, extremely high mp (~3500°C), no electrical conductivity (all electrons in bonds).
✏️ Graphite
Element: Carbon only (allotrope of diamond).
Bonding: Each C bonds to three adjacent C atoms. Fourth electron is delocalised.
Formula: (C)n
Properties: Excellent electrical conductor, soft/slippery (layers slide), used in electrodes and lubricants.
🏖️ Silica (Sand)
Elements: Silicon and oxygen.
Bonding: Each Si bonds to 4 O atoms (similar structure to diamond).
Formula: (SiO₂)n
Properties: Very high mp (1600°C), no conductivity, hard. Main component of sand and glass.
🛡️ Carbides (SiC, B₄C)
Silicon carbide: (SiC)n — used in brake pads, bulletproof vests.
Boron carbide: (B₄C)n — used in armoured vehicles and aircraft.
Properties: Extremely hard ceramic materials, no conductivity, very high melting points.
🔑 Key Properties of All Covalent Networks
Hard: extremely strong covalent bonds throughout — diamond is the hardest known substance.
Very high melting/boiling points: enormous energy needed to break the network of covalent bonds (diamond mp ~4000°C).
No electrical conductivity: all valence electrons are in covalent bonds — none free to move. Exception: graphite (delocalised fourth electron).

Ionic compounds: represented by empirical formulae (simplest ratio). Named: cation name + anion with "-ide".
