Section 1.1

Properties and Uses of Materials

Chemistry is the study of matter and its properties. Matter has mass and takes up space. Properties describe a substance's characteristics and relate directly to the nature and arrangement of its particles.

Physical vs Chemical Properties

⚗️ Physical Properties
Traits that can be observed without a chemical reaction. The substance keeps its identity.

Extensive — change with amount: mass, volume, number of particles.

Intensive — do not change with amount: colour, density, temperature, solubility, melting point, boiling point, conductivity, malleability, ductility, viscosity. These are useful for identifying substances.
🔥 Chemical Properties
Describe how a material undergoes chemical changes. Observing them requires a chemical reaction — the substance changes its identity.

Example: Acidity measures the ability to donate H⁺. Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) is more acidic than water and can dissolve metals — but only by reacting with and consuming itself in the process.

Chemical properties are covered in Topics 5 and 6.

Key Physical Properties

💧
Solubility
The amount of a material (solute) that can dissolve in a given liquid (solvent) at a given temperature, forming a solution. E.g., sugar: 200 g/dL at 25°C. Building materials should have low solubility in water; food packaging should be totally insoluble.
🔥
Thermal Conductivity
How rapidly a material transmits heat by conduction — particles colliding and transferring kinetic energy (like billiard balls). Conductors (metals) transfer heat fast. Insulators transfer heat slowly. A vacuum conducts no heat at all.
⚡
Electrical Conductivity
The ability to carry electricity by moving charged particles. Good conductors: metals (delocalised electrons), ionic solutions. High-resistance conductors: alloys (nichrome). Insulators: gases, glass, rubber, plastic, dry wood.
🧊
Melting Point
The temperature at which a solid transitions to a liquid (particles break out of the rigid lattice). Identical to the freezing point. E.g., water: 0°C; NaCl: 801°C. High melting point materials are used in kilns and furnaces.
♨️
Boiling Point
The temperature at which a liquid transitions to a gas (particles overcome attractive forces). Identical to the condensation point. E.g., water: 100°C; ethanol: 78°C. Coolants need high boiling points to prevent explosion risk.
🌡️
Temperature vs Heat
Heat = total kinetic energy of all particles (extensive). Temperature = average kinetic energy per particle (intensive). Double the water → double the heat energy, but the same temperature.

Types of Electrical Conductors

✅ Good Conductors
Metals — delocalised electrons free to move. Ionic solutions — dissolved ions move toward electrodes. Used in electrical wiring.
🌡️ High-Resistance Conductors
Conduct electricity but charged particles encounter resistance → heat is generated. Metal alloys with high resistance and high melting points. Example: Nichrome (Ni + Cr) in electric oven elements.
🚫 Electrical Insulators
Cannot conduct electricity — no free charged particles. Examples: gases (N₂, O₂), glass, rubber, plastic, dry wood. Used to control and limit current (e.g., plastic coating on wires).
⚠️ Pure Water vs Ionic Solution
Pure water contains no free charges and does not conduct electricity. When ionic substances (e.g., NaCl) dissolve in water, the ions become free to move and the solution conducts electricity. This is why seawater is a good conductor but distilled water is not.
Section 1.1 — Extended

Nanomaterials

Nanomaterials are substances with particles in the size range 1–100 nm. At this scale, materials display dramatically different properties from their bulk forms. "Nano" = one billionth.

🔬
What Makes Them Special?
At the nanoscale, atoms and molecules are large enough to matter individually. The enormous surface area to volume ratio allows far more efficient catalysis of chemical reactions than bulk materials.
💡
OLED Screens
Nanostructured polymer films. Each pixel emits red, green, or blue light independently — allowing true black (pixel off = no light emitted) unlike LCD backlights.
💧
Water Filtration
Nanoscale filters can absorb and remove large amounts of pollutants from drinking water. Large "sponges" absorb many times their weight in oil for oil spill cleanup.
🩺
Medical Uses
Silver nanoparticles kill bacteria without harming larger organisms — used in bandages. Iron oxide nanoparticles bind to cancer cells, making tiny tumours visible on MRI scans.
💉
mRNA Vaccines (COVID-19)
Synthetic lipid nanoparticles act as envelopes to deliver mRNA into cells. Without them, the unstable mRNA breaks down before it can initiate an immune response.
🥇
Gold Nanoparticles
Bulk gold is yellow and non-reactive. Gold nanoparticles can appear red, green, or purple depending on size, and can catalyse reactions that vary by exact number of atoms (e.g., Au₂₈ vs Au₃₈).
Section 1.1 — Separation

Pure Substances, Mixtures & Separation

All materials are either pure substances (one type of particle) or mixtures (multiple substances physically combined). Mixtures can be separated by exploiting differences in physical properties.

TypeDescriptionExamples
ElementPure substance — only one type of atomHelium, iron, nitrogen
CompoundPure substance — different atoms chemically bonded in fixed ratiosWater (H₂O), methane (CH₄), NaCl
Homogeneous MixtureThoroughly mixed — same composition throughout, even at molecular levelSaltwater, alloys, air
Heterogeneous MixtureIncompletely mixed — different parts distinguishable in compositionSoil, salad dressing, crude oil, blood

Separation Techniques

🔵 Filtration
Separates heterogeneous mixtures containing solid particles in a liquid. The mixture passes through filter paper — liquid molecules pass through the tiny pores; larger solid grains are trapped. Both solid and liquid are retained and can be recovered.
♨️ Evaporation
Exploits differences in boiling points to separate a liquid solvent from one or more solutes. The mixture is heated to/above the boiling point of the solvent. The solvent evaporates and is lost; the solute remains. Used to recover dissolved salts.
🧪 Simple Distillation
Separates a liquid with a low boiling point from other substances. Heat vaporises the low-boiling component → vapour enters a condenser → cools to liquid (the distillate) → collected. The low-boiling liquid is isolated; other components remain. Example: separating pure water from saltwater.
🏭 Fractional Distillation
Separates liquids with similar boiling points using a fractionating column with increased internal surface area. Greater control over the distillate temperature allows different substances to be collected in separate flasks. Used to separate components of crude oil.

🔑 Key Definitions
Atomic number (Z) = number of protons = identity of the element.
Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons (measured in amu, electrons ignored).
Charge = determined by protons and electrons. Neutral atoms have equal protons and electrons (charges cancel).

Section 1.2 — Spectra

Absorption & Emission Spectra

Electrons exist in discrete energy levels. They can absorb or emit energy only in specific "chunks" (photons) that exactly match the gap between energy levels. This produces characteristic spectra for each element.

🌑 Atomic Absorption
An electron absorbs a photon and jumps to a higher energy shell (excited state). The photon must have exactly the right energy to match the gap — partial absorption is impossible.

When white light passes through elemental gas, specific wavelengths are absorbed → dark lines appear in the spectrum at those wavelengths.

The pattern of dark lines is unique to each element and can be used to identify it.
✨ Atomic Emission
An excited electron returns to its ground state (lower energy shell). It emits a photon with energy equal to the energy that was originally absorbed.

The emitted light separated into wavelengths produces an emission spectrum — bright coloured lines on a dark background.

The wavelengths in an emission spectrum are identical to those in the absorption spectrum for the same element.

The Visible Spectrum

400 nm (violet, 3.1 eV) 500 nm (green) 600 nm (orange) 700 nm (red, 1.8 eV)

Photon energy is inversely proportional to wavelength. Short wavelength = high energy = violet/purple. Long wavelength = low energy = red.

🌟 Applications of Spectroscopy
Spectroscopic methods have been used to: study the composition of distant stars, monitor dissolved oxygen in blood and aquatic ecosystems, detect toxic compounds in food, identify elements in exoplanetary atmospheres (transit spectroscopy), and detect explosives at airports (mass spectrometry).
Section 1.2 — Isotopes

Isotopes & Atomic Notation

Individual atoms are represented using a composite symbol showing the elemental symbol, mass number (A) and atomic number (Z). Isotopes are versions of an element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

Example — Calcium-42
Ca-42: Z = 20 (20 protons, all calcium), A = 42. Neutrons = 42 − 20 = 22 neutrons. Isotopes of calcium have the same chemical properties (same electrons) but different masses.
⚠️ Isotopes — Same Chemistry, Different Mass
Isotopes have identical chemical properties because chemical behaviour is determined by electrons, and isotopes have the same number of electrons. They differ only in mass (different neutrons) and in their physical properties. Unstable isotopes can decay and emit radiation. Elements 1–42, 44–60, and 62–82 have at least one stable isotope.

⭐ Special Cases — Chromium & Copper
Chromium (Cr, Z=24): ends in 4s¹3d⁵ (not 4s²3d⁴). One electron moves from 4s to 3d because a half-filled d subshell (d⁵) is especially stable.

Copper (Cu, Z=29): ends in 4s¹3d¹⁰ (not 4s²3d⁹). One electron moves from 4s to 3d because a fully-filled d subshell (d¹⁰) is especially stable.

Transition metal ions lose electrons from the ns subshell BEFORE the (n−1)d subshell. E.g., Ni²⁺ loses both 4s electrons, not 3d electrons.
Section 1.3

Quantities of Atoms — The Mole

Atoms are too small to count individually. The mole is a unit that converts between numbers of particles and measurable masses. 1 mole = 6.02 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number, NA).

The Mole Equation
n = m / M
The number of moles is the mass divided by the molar mass.

m

🥚
Avogadro's Number
1 mole = 6.02 × 10²³ particles. Like counting eggs in dozens. Based on the number of carbon atoms in exactly 12.00 g of carbon-12. Named after Amadeo Avogadro.
⚖️
Molar Mass (M)
Mass of one mole of a substance in g/mol. For an element, it equals the atomic mass on the periodic table. For compounds, add up the molar masses of all atoms: M(H₂O) = 2(1.01) + 16.00 = 18.02 g/mol.
🔢
Relative Atomic Mass (Ar)
A weighted average of all isotope masses, weighted by natural abundance on Earth. E.g., bromine: 50.7% Br-79 (78.92 amu) + 49.3% Br-81 (80.92 amu) = Ar ≈ 79.91. This is what appears on the periodic table.
📏
SI Prefixes for Moles
Moles can use SI prefixes: mmol (millimoles, × 10⁻³), μmol (micromoles, × 10⁻⁶), nmol (nanomoles, × 10⁻⁹). Drug doses (e.g., paclitaxel chemotherapy ~12 μmol per dose) are often calculated in micromoles.
Worked Example 1 — Moles of Iron
Iron nail, m = 0.214 g, M(Fe) = 55.85 g/mol
n = m/M = 0.214 / 55.85 = 3.83 × 10⁻³ mol
Worked Example 2 — Methane
m = 1.000 kg = 1000 g, M(CH₄) = 12.01 + 4(1.01) = 16.05 g/mol
n(CH₄) = 1000 / 16.05 = 62.33 mol
n(H atoms) = 62.33 × 4 = 249.3 mol H
Section 1.4

The Periodic Table

118 elements arranged in order of increasing atomic number. Periods (rows) = main electron shells. Groups (columns) = similar electron configurations and properties. The table is divided into s, p, d, and f blocks.

BlockGroupsHighest subshellNames
s-blockI–IIs¹ or s²Group I = alkali metals; Group II = alkaline earth metals. (He is placed in Group XVIII)
p-blockXIII–XVIIIp¹ to p⁶Post-transition metals, metalloids, non-metals. Group XVII = halogens (p⁵). Group XVIII = noble gases (p⁶)
d-blockIII–XIId¹ to d¹⁰Transition metals
f-blockBelow tablef electronsLanthanides (57–71) and Actinides (89–103). Shown separately to save space.
📍 How to Find Period and Group from Electron Configuration
Period = the highest main shell number in the electron configuration. E.g., phosphorus 1s²2s²2p⁶3s²3p³ → Period 3.

Group (main group) = number of electrons in the outermost shell. E.g., phosphorus main-shell config 2,8,5 → Main Group V.

Block = determined by the highest occupied subshell type (s, p, d, or f).
Section 1.4 — Trends

Periodic Trends

The position of an element in the periodic table is related to its physical and chemical properties. Three key trends run predictably across periods and down groups.

⭕ Atomic Radius
Down a group: radius increases — electrons occupy higher shells, further from nucleus.

Across a period (left → right): radius decreases — more protons attract all electrons closer to nucleus (same shell, stronger pull).
↓ group = bigger → period = smaller
🔋 Electronegativity
The ability of an atom to attract electrons to itself (Pauling scale, 0–4). Fluorine = 4.0 (highest). Caesium/Francium = 0.7 (lowest).

Down a group: decreases — larger radius + more shielding electrons weaken attractive force.

Across a period: increases — smaller radius means outermost electrons are held more strongly.
↓ group = lower → period = higher
🔗 Valency
The capacity of an atom to form bonds with other atoms. For main groups I–III and V–VII, the ionic charge can be predicted:

Group I = +1 · Group II = +2 · Group III = +3
Group V = −3 · Group VI = −2 · Group VII = −1
🔩 Metallic Character
Metals: left side of table. Lustrous, good conductors, ductile/malleable, high mp/bp, low electronegativity, form positive cations.

Non-metals: right side. Dull, poor conductors, brittle, low mp/bp, high electronegativity, form negative anions.

Metalloids: straddle the boundary — exhibit both metallic and non-metallic properties.
↕↔ Summary of Trends
Atomic radius and electronegativity are inverse trends — when one increases, the other decreases. Down a group: radius ↑, electronegativity ↓. Across a period (→): radius ↓, electronegativity ↑. Metallic character mirrors the atomic radius trend (larger atoms tend to be more metallic).
